Canadian consumer debt climbed to a record **$2.66 trillion** in the first quarter of 2026, according to Equifax Canada's latest consumer credit report — and beneath that headline number is a story of households under real pressure. Total balances rose 3.8% year over year, yet consumer insolvencies leapt 18.8% to their highest level since 2009. In other words, Canadians are borrowing more in aggregate while a growing share are quietly buckling under what they already owe.

For everyday borrowers, the report released on May 26, 2026 is less about the trillion-dollar totals and more about a simple warning: the cost of carrying debt is catching up with people. Here is what the numbers actually say — and what they mean for how you should borrow this year.

## Canadian Consumer Debt Hits a Record $2.66 Trillion

The topline figure is eye-watering. Total **Canadian consumer debt** reached $2.66 trillion, up 3.8% from a year earlier, driven mostly by mortgages and population growth. But the more interesting movement is underneath: non-mortgage debt — think credit cards, lines of credit, auto loans and personal loans — actually *fell* by more than $487 million in the quarter. That is the first quarterly decline in non-mortgage balances in several quarters, and it suggests many households are consciously pulling back, paying down cards and thinking twice before they spend.

Equifax framed this as "financial discipline" offsetting a difficult macroeconomic backdrop, and the data supports that read. Canadians, on the whole, are trying to get their balances under control. The problem is that discipline arrives too late for a rising number of borrowers who are already over-extended.

## Insolvencies Reach Their Highest Level Since 2009

The 18.8% year-over-year jump in consumer insolvencies is the number that should stop you. The last time filings ran this high was 2009, in the aftermath of the global financial crisis. Back then the trigger was mass job losses; this time it is the slow grind of high carrying costs, elevated interest rates and mortgages renewing at far steeper payments than borrowers signed up for.

The depth of the strain is visible in how much insolvent Canadians owe. The average non-mortgage debt among people filing for insolvency rose to **$43,300**, up from $40,200 just two years ago. For insolvent homeowners the figure is far larger — **$82,400**, a 19% increase. These are not people who overspent on a single vacation. They are households that layered balances on top of balances until the monthly minimums no longer fit the budget.

## Homeowners Are Feeling the Squeeze

Rising **Canadian consumer debt** is often assumed to be a renter's or a young person's problem. This report says otherwise. Mortgage delinquencies surged roughly 52% in Ontario and about 36% in British Columbia — the two provinces where home prices, and therefore mortgage sizes, are highest. When a large mortgage renews at a higher rate, the extra hundreds of dollars a month have to come from somewhere, and for many families that means falling behind elsewhere first.

There is one encouraging detail in the homeowner data. More than **90% of homeowners** who became insolvent chose a *consumer proposal* rather than outright bankruptcy. A consumer proposal is a formal, legally binding arrangement to repay creditors part of what is owed over up to five years, usually while keeping key assets like the home. That so many are choosing the structured route over bankruptcy suggests borrowers are looking for a manageable path out rather than a hard reset — exactly the mindset that keeps a difficult situation from becoming a catastrophe.

## Credit Is Getting Tighter

Lenders read the same data you do, and they are reacting. New credit-card originations fell to a **four-year low** in Q1 2026, and lenders cut average credit limits by **15% to 20%** for borrowers they consider higher risk. Practically, that means two things for the average Canadian. First, the easy credit that papered over tight months in previous years is thinning out. Second, if your credit profile is anything less than pristine, the limit you are offered — and the rate attached to it — is likely to be less generous than it would have been a year ago.

![A credit card close-up as lenders tighten limits amid record Canadian consumer debt and falling card originations](https://images.pexels.com/photos/210742/pexels-photo-210742.jpeg?auto=compress&cs=tinysrgb&w=1200)

This is why leaning on credit cards to bridge a shortfall is a riskier plan in 2026 than it looked in 2023. A shrinking limit can push your utilization ratio up even if you spend nothing more, and higher utilization drags on your credit score — the very number lenders check when you eventually need to borrow for something that matters.

## What Rising Debt Means for Everyday Borrowers

None of this means you should never borrow. It means you should borrow *deliberately*. A smaller, well-structured loan that lowers your total monthly cost is a completely different animal from a new credit line that adds to the pile. Here is how to stay on the right side of that line as insolvencies climb.

### Watch Your Debt-to-Income Ratio

Your debt-to-income (DTI) ratio — the share of your gross monthly income that goes to debt payments — is the single best early-warning signal you have. Lenders generally get nervous above roughly 40%, and so should you. If a new payment would push you past that, it is a sign to borrow less, choose a longer term to lower the payment, or hold off entirely. Our guide to [understanding your debt-to-income ratio](/resources/guides/understanding-debt-to-income-ratio) walks through how to calculate yours in a few minutes and what a healthy number looks like.

### Consider Consolidation to Simplify Payments

If you are already juggling several balances at high interest rates, consolidation is often the most sensible response to this climate. A [debt consolidation loan](/resources/guides/debt-consolidation-guide) replaces multiple payments — cards, store financing, small loans — with a single fixed monthly payment, ideally at a lower rate than the cards it clears. It will not make debt disappear, but it simplifies your finances, can cut the total interest you pay, and dramatically reduces the odds of a missed payment that snowballs. For a household edging toward the strain the Equifax numbers describe, a clean consolidation is a far better outcome than sliding toward a proposal or bankruptcy.

## The Bottom Line

The Q1 2026 data paints a two-speed picture: disciplined Canadians paying down cards on one side, a record number of insolvencies on the other. Which group you end up in has less to do with the economy than with how you manage what you owe over the next year. Keep your debt-to-income ratio in a comfortable range, consolidate high-interest balances before they compound, and treat every new loan as a decision rather than a reflex. With **Canadian consumer debt** at record highs and credit tightening, a smaller, well-structured loan you can clearly afford will always beat the slow drift toward insolvency.
