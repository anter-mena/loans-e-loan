No drama, and no surprise: **the Bank of Canada holds rates steady at 2.25% following its July 15, 2026 decision**, keeping the Bank Rate at 2.5% and the deposit rate at 2.20%. The decision itself was the least interesting part of the day. What actually matters for anyone carrying debt — or thinking about borrowing — is the calendar. The next rate announcement is not until Wednesday, September 2, 2026, and the Bank's own forecast describes a slow-motion year, with growth of just 0.7% expected for 2026. That six-week wait, and what happens at the end of it, is the real story for Canadian borrowers.

![Financial documents and rate charts on a desk after the Bank of Canada held its policy rate at 2.25% in July 2026](https://images.pexels.com/photos/4386339/pexels-photo-4386339.jpeg?auto=compress&cs=tinysrgb&w=1200)

## Bank of Canada holds rates: what was decided July 15

Nobody in the market was caught off guard by the hold. Inflation has been drifting back toward target — [June's reading came in at 2.8%](/news/canada-inflation-june-2026-cpi) — and the economy has been too soft to justify a hike, but not so weak that the Bank felt forced to cut. The more revealing document was the accompanying Monetary Policy Report, which describes an economy that has been weak but is showing signs of improvement: growth of just 0.7% expected for 2026 overall, picking up to 1.8% in both 2027 and 2028, with inflation projected to ease to around 2%.

Here is the decision and the forecast at a glance:

| Bank of Canada, July 15, 2026 | Detail |
|---|---|
| Policy (overnight) rate | 2.25% — unchanged |
| Bank Rate | 2.50% |
| Deposit rate | 2.20% |
| Growth forecast, 2026 | 0.7% |
| Growth forecast, 2027 and 2028 | 1.8% each year |
| Inflation outlook | Easing to around 2% |
| Next rate announcement | Wednesday, September 2, 2026 |

Read that table as the Bank telling Canadians three things at once: this year will be slow, better days are pencilled in for 2027, and it is not moving until the data forces its hand.

## Why a hold means no imminent relief

A hold is not a neutral event for borrowers — it locks in the status quo for at least six more weeks. Variable-rate products, from variable mortgages to lines of credit, are priced off lenders' prime rates, and prime tracks the Bank's policy rate. When the policy rate does not move, prime does not move, and neither does the variable side of the market. With no announcement scheduled before September 2, there is simply no mechanism for broad rate relief between now and then.

The mortgage market already reflects that reality. As of mid-July:

| Mortgage market snapshot, mid-July 2026 | Where things stand |
|---|---|
| Lowest variable mortgage rates | Around 3.3% |
| Lowest fixed mortgage rates | Around 3.9% |
| Expected movement before September 2 | Little — variable rates track the policy rate |

Could September bring a cut? The Bank's own 0.7% growth forecast describes an economy limping through 2026, and a soft economy with inflation heading toward 2% is the kind of backdrop that eventually opens the door to easing. But the Bank promised nothing on July 15, and the word eventually is doing a lot of work in that sentence. Planning your finances around a specific cut on a specific date is speculation, not budgeting.

## What it means for personal-loan borrowers

If you already have a fixed-rate personal loan, this announcement changes nothing. Your rate and payment were locked in the moment you signed, and they stay locked no matter what the Bank does in September. If you have a variable-rate loan or line of credit, your rate holds steady too — for now — because prime is not moving before the next announcement. That difference in behaviour is exactly why it pays to understand [fixed versus variable rates](/resources/comparisons/fixed-vs-variable-rates) before you borrow, especially in a stretch where the policy path is genuinely uncertain.

For anyone shopping for a new loan, keep the policy rate in perspective. The Bank of Canada sets the floor for the cost of money in this country, but the rate you are actually offered is dominated by your credit score, your income, your existing debts and your choice of lender. The spread between what a strong applicant and a weak applicant pay is measured in whole percentage points — far more than any single Bank of Canada move could ever deliver. ## Should you wait for September 2?

For most borrowers with a genuine need, no. Start with what waiting actually buys you. If the Bank cuts in September — which it has not promised — variable rates would drift down modestly, while fixed rates may barely react at all, since they already reflect market expectations about where policy is heading. Meanwhile, whatever you needed the money for does not pause. The balance you meant to consolidate keeps charging interest, the repair bill does not shrink, and the six weeks pass either way.

The smarter play is to act on the things you control. Compare offers from several lenders rather than accepting the first quote. Choose a fixed rate if certainty matters to you — it is immune to whatever September brings. Keep the amount modest and make sure the payment is comfortable at today's rates; if a cut arrives later, treat it as a bonus, not a rescue. And if your credit profile needs work, a few months of on-time payments and lower balances can move your next offer more than the Bank will.

## The bottom line

The July 15 hold at 2.25% told Canadians what they already suspected: the Bank of Canada is waiting, and borrowers will be waiting with it until at least September 2. The Bank's own forecast — 0.7% growth this year, inflation settling around 2% — sketches a slow but stabilizing economy, not one on the verge of dramatic rate moves in either direction.
