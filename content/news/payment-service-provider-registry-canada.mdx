**Payment service provider registry Canada** entries are becoming more useful: the Bank of Canada announced that publishable violations will also be noted on a provider's record. That creates another verification step for payment and money-transfer apps. It does **not** mean every registered provider is endorsed, risk-free or covered by deposit insurance.

*Published July 20, 2026. This article explains public regulatory information and is not a recommendation of any payment app, lender or account.*

## Quick answer: what the Bank changed

On June 12, the [Bank of Canada announced](https://www.bankofcanada.ca/2026/06/bank-canada-begin-publishing-notices-violation-payment-service-providers/) that it would begin publishing Notices of Violation involving payment service providers subject to the Retail Payment Activities Act.

After a provider receives a notice and the period for making representations expires, the enforcement page may identify the provider, the nature of the violation and any administrative monetary penalty. The violation will also be marked on that provider's public registry entry.

The timing matters. A notice may not appear immediately, and an empty violations field is not proof that a service has never had complaints, outages or problems outside the Bank's mandate.

## Payment service provider registry Canada: 7 checks

1. **Search both names.** Look up the app's brand and the legal company name. Similar names and copied logos are common warning signs.
2. **Match the website and contact details.** Use information reached from the [official Bank registry](https://www.bankofcanada.ca/core-functions/retail-payments-supervision/psp-registry/), not a link sent in an unexpected text.
3. **Read the violations field.** If an entry shows a violation, open the enforcement decision and note the conduct, date and penalty instead of judging from the label alone.
4. **Check enforcement separately.** The Bank's [enforcement decisions page](https://www.bankofcanada.ca/regulatory-oversight/retail-payments/enforcement-decisions/) can include temporary orders and other regulatory records.
5. **Confirm who holds customer funds.** Ask whether funds are held directly by the PSP or at another institution, and in whose name.
6. **Verify insurance claims.** A registry entry is not proof of CDIC protection. Check the institution and deposit category against [CDIC coverage rules](https://www.cdic.ca/depositors/whats-covered/).
7. **Review exit risk.** Before depositing money, find withdrawal limits, timing, fees, complaint channels and what happens if access is frozen.

This process also helps when evaluating a money app attached to borrowing. Our [Canadian money-app guide](/blog/apps-like-cash-app-canada) covers identity, function and fee checks; the PSP registry answers a narrower payment-regulation question.

## What registration proves—and what it does not

The Bank says registered PSPs have met the legal registration requirements, paid the required fees and cleared a national-security screening coordinated by the Department of Finance. Registered providers are subject to risk supervision and enforcement.

The same registry page explicitly says the Bank does **not** endorse or license registered PSPs. Registration does not tell you that:

- an app is the cheapest option;
- transfers can never be delayed or reversed;
- every balance has deposit insurance;
- a linked lender is licensed in your province;
- a provider's privacy practices fit your needs; or
- customer service will resolve a dispute quickly.

The distinction prevents a regulatory fact from becoming a misleading marketing claim. If an app advertises “Bank of Canada approved,” compare that wording with the Bank's own disclaimer.

## A safe-app decision rule

Use three separate questions before trusting a service:

| Question | Best evidence |
|---|---|
| Is the payment provider registered? | Bank of Canada PSP registry |
| Are deposited funds insured? | CDIC member and coverage records |
| Is a loan legal and affordable? | Provincial licence, written APR, fees and total repayment |

No single database answers all three. If an app also offers credit, avoid submitting repeated applications merely to “see your rate”; our [hard-credit-check report](/news/canada-hard-credit-checks-2026) explains when an inquiry may affect a credit file. Compare an app's real function and fees with our [Nyble alternatives review](/blog/apps-like-nyble-canada).

Pause if the company demands remote access to your phone, asks for a password or one-time security code, guarantees a loan before review, or requires an advance payment by crypto or gift card.

## Bottom line

The violation update makes the **payment service provider registry Canada** a better due-diligence tool. Search legal and trade names, read any decision, verify where funds sit and check deposit insurance independently. Registration is meaningful regulatory information—but it is one layer of a consumer decision, not a safety seal.
