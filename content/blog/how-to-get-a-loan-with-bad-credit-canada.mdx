If you have been turned down by your bank, learning **how to get a loan with bad credit in Canada** is less about finding a secret lender and more about knowing which doors are actually open to you. A low score narrows your options, but it rarely closes them all — secured loans, credit unions, co-signer loans, and alternative installment lenders exist precisely for borrowers the big banks pass over. This 2026 guide walks through what "bad credit" really means, the loan types that work when your score is low, how to improve your odds before you apply, what it should cost, and the scams to steer well clear of.

## Quick Answer

You get a loan with bad credit by matching yourself to a lender built for your situation, not one that will decline you. In order of accessibility, that usually means a **secured loan** (backed by collateral, so your score matters less), a **guarantor or co-signer loan**, a **credit union** (often more flexible than a bank), or an **alternative/subprime installment lender** that weighs your income and affordability over your score. Boost your odds by showing stable income, asking for less, lowering your existing debt, and fixing errors on your report. Expect a higher rate — but never above the legal **35% APR** cap — and walk away from any "guaranteed approval" offer that asks for an upfront fee.

This article is general information, not financial advice. For guidance tailored to your situation, speak with a licensed financial advisor or a non-profit credit counsellor.

## What Counts as "Bad Credit" in Canada

In Canada, your credit score comes from two bureaus — **Equifax** and **TransUnion** — and runs from **300 to 900**. There is no single official line where "good" becomes "bad," but lenders group scores into bands and most treat anything below roughly **600** as high-risk.

| Score range | How lenders read it | What it usually means for borrowing |
|---|---|---|
| 760–900 | Excellent | Best rates, easy approval |
| 725–759 | Very good | Strong odds at competitive rates |
| 660–724 | Good | Approved by most mainstream lenders |
| 560–659 | Fair / below average | "Bad credit" territory; banks get cautious |
| 300–559 | Poor | Secured, credit-builder, and subprime routes |

A low score usually comes from one or more of the usual culprits: **missed or late payments**, **high credit utilization** (balances close to your limits), **debts sent to collections**, past insolvency, or simply a **thin file** — too little history for a lender to judge you, which catches out newcomers and young adults who have done nothing wrong.

The takeaway is that "bad credit" is a range, not a verdict. A 540 score with a steady paycheque and little debt is a very different application from a 540 buried under maxed-out cards. Before you apply, pull your report from both bureaus and read it closely — our guide on [understanding credit reports](/resources/guides/understanding-credit-reports) shows how, and the Government of Canada's [credit reports and scores](https://www.canada.ca/en/financial-consumer-agency/services/credit-reports-score.html) resource from the Financial Consumer Agency of Canada (FCAC) explains each factor.

## How to Get a Loan With Bad Credit in Canada: The Options That Work

Here is the honest hierarchy. Mainstream banks price almost entirely on your score, so with bad credit they usually say no. The lenders below are built differently — they lean on collateral, a co-signer, a membership relationship, or a close look at your income instead.

| Loan option | What it typically needs | Typical cost / rate | Best suited to |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Secured loan** | Collateral: car, savings, or home equity | Lower than unsecured; capped at 35% APR | Owning an asset you can pledge |
| **Co-signer / guarantor loan** | A creditworthy backer who shares liability | Often close to the co-signer's rate | Having someone who trusts you |
| **Credit union loan** | Membership + proof of steady income | Frequently below subprime pricing | People who value flexibility |
| **Alternative / subprime installment** | Proof of stable income & affordability | Higher APR, hard-capped at 35% | Score-flexible, income-focused approval |
| **Credit-builder loan** | Small, regular fixed payments | Modest interest + small fees | Building history before you borrow more |
| **Payday loan (last resort)** | Income + a bank account | Very expensive per-$100 fee | Avoiding if at all possible |

### Secured loans

A secured loan is the most reliable way to borrow with a low score. You pledge an asset — a vehicle, a savings deposit, or home equity — and because the lender can recover it if you default, your score matters far less, which usually means a **lower rate** than an unsecured loan. The trade-off is real: miss payments and you can lose the collateral, so borrow only what your budget comfortably supports. Our primer on [secured vs unsecured loans](/resources/guides/secured-vs-unsecured-loans-guide) explains the mechanics.

### Guarantor and co-signer loans

If you lack collateral but have someone with good credit willing to help, a **co-signer or guarantor loan** can unlock approval. The co-signer agrees to repay if you cannot, which reassures the lender and often earns you a rate closer to their credit than yours. It is a serious ask, though — a missed payment hurts both of you. Make sure everyone understands the commitment first; our guide to [cosigner responsibilities](/resources/guides/cosigner-responsibilities) lays out what they are signing up for.

### Credit unions

**Credit unions** are member-owned and tend to decide with more human judgment than a big bank's automated scorecard. Because you are a member rather than an account number, they can weigh your relationship, income stability, and your story — not only a three-digit score. A local credit union you can join is one of the most underrated first stops for a bad-credit borrower. Bring proof of steady income and be ready to explain what dented your credit and what has changed since.

### Alternative and subprime installment lenders

A large market of **alternative and subprime installment lenders** specialises in exactly this situation. They approve based on your **income and affordability** — can you comfortably make the payment? — far more than on your score. The loans are fixed monthly installments over a set term, which makes budgeting predictable, and on-time payments are reported to the bureaus, so they double as a credit-building tool. You will pay more than a prime borrower, but a legitimate lender's rate is always **at or below 35% APR**. ### Credit-builder loans and secured cards

If you can wait a little, a **credit-builder loan** flips borrowing on its head: your payments go into a locked savings account, and you receive the money (minus small fees) at the end, having built a wall of on-time payments along the way. Paired with a **secured credit card**, it is the cheapest route to a stronger score — which then unlocks better loan terms. This is the tool of choice when your need is not urgent and your real goal is to stop being a "bad credit" borrower at all.

## How to Boost Your Approval Odds

Which lender you approach matters, but so does how you show up. These moves improve your chances and often win a better rate:

- **Show stable income.** Nothing reassures a lender more than proof you can make the payment — have recent pay stubs or bank statements ready.
- **Ask for a smaller amount.** A modest request is easier to approve and to repay, so borrow what you truly need, not the maximum offered.
- **Add a co-signer.** If a trusted person with good credit will back you, it can turn a "no" into a "yes" and lower your rate.
- **Lower your existing debt.** Your **debt-to-income ratio** — how much of your income already goes to debt payments — is make-or-break for many lenders. Paying down a balance before you apply signals room for the new payment; our guide on [understanding debt-to-income ratio](/resources/guides/understanding-debt-to-income-ratio) shows how to improve yours.
- **Fix errors on your report.** A payment wrongly marked late, or a debt that should read "paid," can drag your score down unfairly. Dispute mistakes with Equifax and TransUnion before you apply.
- **Trim your credit utilization.** Getting balances below about 30% of your limits can nudge your score up quickly; our explainer on [credit utilization](/resources/guides/credit-utilization-explained) covers the fastest wins.
- **Apply selectively.** Every hard inquiry dings your score, so target one or two lenders that work with bad credit rather than scattering applications.

## Rates and the 35% Cap

Borrowing with bad credit costs more — that is the price of the extra risk a lender takes on. The key is knowing where the legal line sits.

As of **January 1, 2025, the criminal rate of interest in Canada is capped at 35% APR** (annual percentage rate). Any loan priced above that ceiling is illegal, full stop. Two points make this useful:

1. **APR includes fees, not just the headline rate.** A "low rate" wrapped in heavy processing or "insurance" fees can quietly push the true cost toward the cap, so always ask for the all-in APR.
2. **A higher rate is not automatically a bad loan.** A subprime installment loan at, say, 29% that you repay on time is a legitimate rebuilding tool. What matters is that the payment fits your budget.

## Scams to Avoid

Bad-credit borrowers are the number-one target for predatory operators, because scammers assume desperation makes people skip their guard. Rising consumer debt makes the problem worse, as our roundup on [Canada's consumer debt and insolvency trends](/news/canadian-consumer-debt-insolvencies-2026) explains. Watch for these red flags:

- **"Guaranteed approval, no credit check."** No legitimate lender guarantees a loan without assessing you. This is the most common lure aimed squarely at bad credit.
- **Upfront or advance fees.** If you are asked to pay an "insurance," "processing," or "release" fee *before* the loan is funded — especially by e-transfer, gift card, or crypto — it is an **advance-fee scam**. Real lenders deduct fees from the loan or bill them afterward.
- **Rates above 35% APR.** Illegal by definition. If the all-in cost tops the cap, walk away.
- **Pressure and secrecy.** "Act now," refusal to put terms in writing, or a lender with no verifiable Canadian address are all warning signs.

Be especially wary of offers marketed as [no credit check loans](/resources/guides/no-credit-check-loans) — some are legitimate income-based products, but the phrase is also scam bait, so vet the lender carefully. ## Rebuilding Your Credit

Getting the loan is only half the win. The bigger goal is a credit profile strong enough that you never borrow as a "bad credit" customer again. A simple, disciplined routine does most of the work:

1. **Pay on time, every time.** Payment history is the largest factor in your score, so set up autopay for at least the minimum.
2. **Keep utilization low.** Aim for balances under about 30% of each limit, ideally under 10% — high balances hurt even when you pay them off.
3. **Add a secured card or credit-builder loan.** A small, well-managed tradeline builds positive history fast, especially if your file is thin.
4. **Check your report regularly.** Dispute anything wrong. If old collections are dragging you down, our guide on [rebuilding credit after collections](/resources/guides/how-to-rebuild-credit-after-collections) covers how to handle them.
5. **Build a small cushion.** A modest emergency fund keeps the next surprise bill from forcing you into expensive borrowing; our guide on [emergency fund basics](/resources/guides/emergency-fund-basics) shows how to start with a few dollars a week.

If you are climbing back from a deeper setback, our guide on [loans for discharged bankrupts in Canada](/blog/loans-for-discharged-bankrupts-canada) maps the realistic ladder from a secured card to mainstream rates. Stick to the routine and most people see meaningful improvement within a year.
