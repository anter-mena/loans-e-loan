If your bankruptcy is behind you, **loans for discharged bankrupts in Canada** are far more within reach than most people assume. Discharge is the finish line, not a life sentence — it means the legal process is complete, your eligible unsecured debts are cleared, and you are free to borrow and rebuild. The catch is that the record of your bankruptcy lingers on your credit file for years, so approvals come back gradually rather than all at once. This guide explains exactly what "discharged" changes, which loans you can realistically get and when, how to boost your odds, and the scams to avoid while you climb back.

## Quick Answer

Once you are discharged, no law stops any lender from approving you — the decision is purely commercial. Start with credit-building tools you can almost always get right away: a **secured credit card** and a **credit-builder loan**. Within months, **alternative and subprime installment lenders** and **RRSP loans** open up, followed by **secured car and home loans**. Mainstream bank rates return last, usually **two to four years after discharge**, once you have rebuilt a positive payment history and the bankruptcy has aged on your report. The single biggest lever in your control is proving you can make **affordable, on-time payments** — that, more than time itself, is what turns a "no" into a "yes."

This article is general information, not financial or legal advice. For guidance tailored to your situation, speak with a Licensed Insolvency Trustee (LIT) or a non-profit credit counsellor.

## What "Discharged" Actually Changes

Bankruptcy in Canada is governed by the **Bankruptcy and Insolvency Act (BIA)** and can only be filed through a **Licensed Insolvency Trustee**, with the **Office of the Superintendent of Bankruptcy (OSB)** overseeing the system. A **first-time bankruptcy with no surplus income is discharged automatically at around nine months** (about 21 months if you have surplus income). Discharge is the moment the process legally ends and your eligible unsecured debts — credit cards, most personal loans, lines of credit — are wiped clean.

For borrowing, discharge changes three things:

- **You are legally free to apply again.** There is no rule barring a discharged bankrupt from taking a loan. Lenders set their own policies.
- **Your debt load is gone.** Clearing that debt often improves your debt-to-income ratio overnight, which some lenders weigh heavily.
- **You have a clean slate to build on.** Every on-time payment from here forward is fresh, positive history that pushes the bankruptcy further into the past.

What discharge does *not* do is erase the bankruptcy from your credit report. That is the piece most people misunderstand, and it is worth understanding in detail.

## How Long a Discharged Bankruptcy Stays on Your Credit

A bankruptcy is recorded on your credit file with an **R9 rating** — the worst rating there is. After discharge, a **first bankruptcy generally stays on your Equifax report for about six years from the discharge date**. On TransUnion it is broadly similar, roughly **six years, and up to seven in some provinces**. A second bankruptcy stays on file much longer (commonly 14 years on Equifax).

The good news: when the reporting period ends, the record **drops off automatically**. You do not have to apply, pay, or ask a bureau to remove it. Here is the typical timeline:

| Time since discharge | What the record looks like | What you can usually get |
|---|---|---|
| 0–6 months | Bankruptcy freshly discharged, R9 on file | Secured credit card, credit-builder loan |
| 6–24 months | Some positive new tradelines building | Subprime/alternative installment loans, RRSP loan, secured car loan |
| 2–4 years | Solid rebuilt history, bankruptcy aging | Better-rate installment loans, some prime products, mortgages with conditions |
| ~6 years | Bankruptcy drops off automatically | Full access to mainstream rates (subject to your current profile) |

The key insight is that a discharged bankrupt is not stuck for the full six years. Lenders care as much about the *trend* — a rising score and a wall of recent on-time payments — as they do about the old flag. Two years of clean history can outweigh a bankruptcy still technically on file. Pull your report from both bureaus to confirm your exact discharge date and that the entry is accurate; our guide on [understanding credit reports](/resources/guides/understanding-credit-reports) explains how.

## Loans for Discharged Bankrupts in Canada: What You Can Actually Get

Here is the realistic ladder, from what you can get on day one to what returns last. Climb it in order — each rung builds the history that unlocks the next.

| Product | When it's realistic | Typical cost | Why it works |
|---|---|---|---|
| Secured credit card | Right away | Small annual fee; you post a deposit | Deposit removes lender risk; reports to bureaus |
| Credit-builder loan | Right away | Modest interest + small fees | You "repay" into savings; every payment reports |
| RRSP loan | A few months | Lower rates (loan is secured by the investment) | Backed by the RRSP contribution you're funding |
| Secured car loan | 3–12 months | Higher APR than prime, but reasonable | Vehicle acts as collateral |
| Alternative/subprime installment loan | Within months | Higher APR, capped at 35% | Lenders that specialise in rebuilding credit |
| Prime bank personal loan | 2–4 years | Best available rates | Requires rebuilt score and aged discharge |

### Secured credit cards and credit-builder loans

These are the foundation. A **secured credit card** requires a refundable deposit (often $200–$500) that becomes your limit, so the lender takes no risk — which is exactly why a discharged bankrupt can get one immediately. Use it for a small recurring bill, pay it in full monthly, and it reports positive history to the bureaus.

A **credit-builder loan** flips borrowing on its head: the "loan" is held in a locked savings account while you make fixed payments, and you receive the money (minus fees) at the end. You are effectively paying to build a payment record and a little savings at once. Both tools are the fastest, cheapest way to start.

### RRSP loans and secured car or home loans

Because these are **secured** against an asset — your RRSP investment, your vehicle, or home equity — the lender's risk drops sharply, making approval far easier soon after discharge and the rate lower than an unsecured loan; our primer on [secured vs unsecured loans](/resources/guides/secured-vs-unsecured-loans-guide) explains the trade-offs. The catch is real: miss payments and you can lose the asset, so borrow only what the budget comfortably supports.

### Alternative and subprime installment lenders

Many lenders in Canada specialise in borrowers rebuilding after insolvency. Their **alternative or subprime installment loans** carry higher rates than a bank, but they report to the bureaus and give you a fixed, predictable repayment schedule — useful for both a genuine need and for building history. Compare offers rather than taking the first one, and read our guide to [bad credit loans](/resources/guides/bad-credit-loans) so you know what a fair deal looks like. ### Mainstream banks — later

Big-bank personal loans at prime rates are usually the **last** rung. They typically return two to four years after discharge, once your rebuilt score and clean history do the talking. ## How to Boost Your Approval Odds

Time helps, but these moves speed things up and get you a better rate when you do qualify:

- **Show stable income.** Lenders want to see you can service the payment. Steady employment or consistent self-employment income is your strongest asset.
- **Keep your debt-to-income ratio low.** Post-discharge you should have little debt — don't rush to fill it back up. A low ratio signals room to repay.
- **Start small and secured.** A $500 secured card managed perfectly for six months is more persuasive than a large unsecured request that gets declined.
- **Correct report errors.** A discharge wrongly showing as an active bankruptcy, or debts that should read "included in bankruptcy," can drag your score down unfairly. Dispute them.
- **Apply selectively.** Every hard inquiry dings your score. Space out applications and target lenders that work with discharged bankrupts.
- **Bring the paperwork.** Your discharge certificate, recent pay stubs, and a simple budget show a lender you are organised and serious.

## Interest Rates and the 35% Cap

Expect to pay more at first — that is the cost of the risk a lender takes on early. But there is a firm legal ceiling. As of **January 1, 2025, the criminal rate of interest in Canada is capped at 35% APR** (annual percentage rate). Any loan priced above that is illegal, full stop. If you see a rate — or an all-in cost once fees are counted — above 35%, that is not a lender you want.

Two practical points:

1. **APR includes fees, not just the headline rate.** A "low rate" wrapped in heavy fees can quietly push the true APR toward the cap, so always ask for the APR.
2. **A higher rate is not automatically a bad loan.** A subprime installment loan at, say, 29% that you repay on time is a legitimate rebuilding tool. What matters is that the payment fits your budget.

## Scams to Dodge

Discharged bankrupts are prime targets for predatory operators, because scammers assume you are desperate for a "yes." Watch for these red flags:

- **"Guaranteed approval, no credit check."** No legitimate lender guarantees approval sight unseen. This is the single most common lure.
- **Upfront or advance fees.** If you are asked to pay an "insurance," "processing," or "release" fee *before* the loan is funded — especially by e-transfer, gift card, or crypto — it is an advance-fee scam. Real lenders deduct fees from the loan or bill them after funding.
- **Rates above 35% APR.** Illegal by definition.
- **Pressure and secrecy.** "Act now," reluctance to put terms in writing, or a lender with no verifiable Canadian address are all warning signs.

## Rebuilding Credit After Discharge

Getting a loan is only half the goal — the real win is a credit profile strong enough that you never need subprime products again. A simple, disciplined routine does most of the work:

1. **Open one secured card or credit-builder loan** and treat it as sacred. On time, every time.
2. **Keep utilisation low** — under about 30% of your limit, ideally under 10%. High balances hurt even when you pay them off.
3. **Add a second small tradeline after six months** so you have two positive accounts reporting, not one.
4. **Never miss a payment.** A single 30-day late can undo months of progress. Set up autopay for at least the minimum.
5. **Check your report every few months** to confirm accounts are reporting correctly and the bankruptcy is dated right.

If you also have old collections lingering alongside the discharge, our guide on [rebuilding credit after collections](/resources/guides/how-to-rebuild-credit-after-collections) covers how to handle those without hurting your score. Most people who follow this routine see meaningful score gains within a year, and are into mainstream products within two to three.
